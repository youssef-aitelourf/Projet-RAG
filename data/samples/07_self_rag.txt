Self-RAG and Adaptive Retrieval

Self-RAG (Self-Reflective Retrieval-Augmented Generation) trains the language model to decide when retrieval is needed and to critique retrieved evidence and its own outputs using special reflection tokens.

Unlike naive RAG which always retrieves top-k chunks, Self-RAG can skip retrieval for questions answerable from parametric knowledge, saving latency and reducing noise.

Reflection tokens signal actions such as: retrieve, relevance score for a passage, support score for a generated sentence, and overall utility of the response. The model learns these behaviors during fine-tuning with reinforcement learning or supervised data.

Adaptive RAG routes queries to different strategies based on complexity: simple factual questions may use single-hop retrieval; multi-hop questions trigger iterative retrieval or graph-based methods.

Corrective RAG (CRAG) adds a retrieval evaluator that filters or web-searches when retrieved documents are judged irrelevant, reducing propagation of bad context to the generator.

These methods address limitations of static pipelines where retrieval always runs regardless of query type or retrieval quality.

Production consideration: Self-RAG requires model fine-tuning and is not available out-of-the-box with generic API models, but the design principles inform when to add retrieval gates in custom pipelines.
